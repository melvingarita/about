About

Melvin Garita Mora

Banking Executive · Institutional Leadership at Scale

I grew up on Costa Rica’s Caribbean coast and left home at eighteen to study economics. Entering large organizations from the outside shaped how I learned to understand them — first by observing them, and eventually by leading them.

Twenty years later, I lead operations at the country’s largest bank — overseeing 1,700 professionals and the daily execution infrastructure of a USD 17 billion institution.

The distance between those two points is not a story of methodology or frameworks. It is a story of progressive institutional complexity — each role larger, each institution more demanding, each decision carrying greater consequence. The thread that connects them is a consistent focus on one problem: why do institutions with sound strategies still underperform? After two decades, my answer has not changed — it is almost never the strategy. It is the distance between boardroom intent and organizational reality.

Career as Narrative

Four Chapters

Chapter I

The Quantitative Foundation

2004–2015 · Analyst → Trader → Head of Trading Desks

I began in capital markets — analyzing risk, pricing securities, and eventually managing approximately one-third of Banco Nacional’s intermediation economics (~USD 218M annually). A Master’s in Applied Mathematics was not an academic pursuit; it was a deliberate investment in thinking about institutions the way I think about complex systems — with precision, with models, and with a healthy respect for what the models cannot capture. This period built the financial acuity that still informs how I evaluate risk, allocate resources, and assess strategic tradeoffs at enterprise level.

Chapter II

The First Turnaround

2004–2013 · LAFISE Valores / BANEX Valores · Analyst to Interim CEO

Over nearly a decade, I progressed from analyst to leadership at a brokerage firm that would eventually need rescuing. When LAFISE Valores ranked last in the industry by profitability, I led the turnaround — restoring the firm to above-average performance within twelve months, with limited resources and no institutional safety net. It was formative in the most direct sense: I learned that institutional leadership under constraint is fundamentally different from leadership with abundant resources. Discipline replaces ambition as the primary operating principle. That distinction has stayed with me.

Chapter III

The CEO Chapter

2018–2023 · BN Valores · General Manager (CEO)

This was the role that tested whether the principles I had developed could build something durable — not just rescue something broken. Over five years, I led BN Valores from a subsidiary without formal strategic planning to a nationally and internationally recognized benchmark. ROAE improved 56%. The efficiency ratio dropped from 1.13 to 0.77. The organization reached the top decile of McKinsey’s Organizational Health Index in Latin America. It earned the National Excellence Award and international recognition through the IBPC. More importantly, the leadership team I restructured and developed sustained the institution’s performance trajectory beyond my tenure. That, more than any metric, is the measure I care about.

Chapter IV

Institutional Scale

2023–Present · Banco Nacional de Costa Rica · Deputy General Manager

First in strategy, then in operations. Leading at this scale — 1,700+ professionals, ~USD 160 million in annual operating budget, the execution infrastructure of a USD 17 billion institution — changed how I understand leadership. At 71 people, a leader can see every connection between intent and execution. At this scale, the system must carry the discipline. My role became less about directing and more about ensuring the institutional architecture holds — connecting strategic objectives to operational delivery across an organization where misalignment compounds quietly and correction requires patience measured in years.

Academic

Teaching

I serve as Professor of Business Economics in the MBA program at Universidad de Costa Rica, the country’s top-ranked university. In 2025, I received formal recognition for outstanding teaching performance — with student evaluations above 9/10 and a 9.8 rating in subject matter mastery.

Teaching is not separate from leadership — it is an extension of the same conviction: that executive knowledge creates greater institutional value when it can be structured, communicated, and transferred. The discipline required to make complex ideas clear to a classroom is the same discipline required to make strategy legible across an organization.
